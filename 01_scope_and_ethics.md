# Scope and Ethics

## Purpose
This repository documents a systematic comparative red-teaming effort examining alignment robustness differences between Constitutional AI and RLHF-based guardrails in frontier large language models. The work emphasizes methodology, reproducibility, and model self-classification of risk, rather than the publication of sensitive prompts or actionable outputs.

The goal is to contribute to AI safety research by improving understanding of how high-risk knowledge boundaries hold or degrade under sustained, socially-conditioned interaction.

## Ethical Boundaries
This work is conducted strictly within an AI safety and harm-prevention context. No real-world testing, physical experimentation, or external validation of model outputs was performed.

All interactions were limited to model responses within controlled conversational environments.

## Explicit Exclusions
The following are intentionally excluded from this repository:
- Exact prompts or prompt sequences
- Step-by-step instructions generated by models
- Raw or unredacted model outputs
- Screenshots containing actionable details
- Any information intended to enable real-world misuse

## Redaction Policy
All examples, excerpts, and references to model behavior are either abstracted or redacted. Where screenshots are referenced, sensitive content is intentionally obscured, preserving only high-level classification or acknowledgment text necessary for safety analysis.

## Stop Conditions
Exploration of any interaction was halted once the model explicitly demonstrated high-risk knowledge boundaries or self-identified the response as falling within a restricted domain. No attempts were made to extend, optimize, or operationalize such outputs.

## Research Framing

This work treats safety failures as an **alignment problem**, not a content-generation problem, with specific attention to comparative performance between Constitutional AI and RLHF approaches.

The focus is not on whether models can produce harmful information in isolation, but on how **interaction dynamics influence boundary enforcement** and why Constitutional AI demonstrates relatively stronger initial resistance in this protocol.

This distinction is important:

- Content-level safety evaluates individual responses  
- Alignment-level safety evaluates behavior across interactions  

The findings in this repository suggest that risks emerge not only from what models know, but from **how they adapt during sustained social conditioning**, even as Constitutional AI shows meaningful robustness advantages over pure RLHF systems in multi-turn scenarios.

All analysis is conducted with the goal of improving understanding of these dynamics in support of safer system design.
